Web3 Concepts

Side-by-side comparisons of core blockchain and Web3 concepts — explained simply

🌐 Web2 vs Web3

🏢 Web2

  • Controlled by centralized companies (Google, Meta, Amazon)
  • Your data is stored on private servers you don't own
  • Platforms can ban, censor, or delete your account
  • Trust is placed in corporations and intermediaries
  • Monetization flows to platform owners, not creators
  • Single points of failure — if the server goes down, so does the app

🔗 Web3

  • Decentralized — no single entity controls the network
  • You own your data and digital assets via private keys
  • Censorship-resistant — no one can revoke your access
  • Trust is replaced by cryptographic proofs and smart contracts
  • Creators earn directly through tokens and NFTs
  • Distributed nodes — no single point of failure

⚡ Ethereum vs Bitcoin

₿ Bitcoin

  • Created in 2009 by Satoshi Nakamoto
  • Primary purpose: digital store of value ("digital gold")
  • Limited scripting — not designed for complex programs
  • Fixed supply of 21 million BTC
  • Proof of Work consensus — energy intensive
  • ~7 transactions per second on mainnet

Ξ Ethereum

  • Launched in 2015 by Vitalik Buterin
  • Primary purpose: programmable blockchain platform
  • Turing-complete smart contracts via the EVM
  • No hard supply cap — issuance controlled by protocol
  • Proof of Stake consensus — 99.9% less energy
  • ~15–30 TPS on mainnet (thousands with L2s)

🔑 Public Key vs Private Key

🔓 Public Key

  • Derived mathematically from your private key
  • Acts like your bank account number — safe to share
  • Used to receive funds and verify signatures
  • Anyone can encrypt a message using your public key
  • Your wallet address is derived from your public key
  • Losing it is not a problem — it can be regenerated

🔐 Private Key

  • A secret 256-bit number — never share it with anyone
  • Acts like your PIN + password combined
  • Used to sign transactions and prove ownership
  • Only you can decrypt messages encrypted with your public key
  • Controls all assets in your wallet
  • Losing it means permanent loss of access to your funds

🗄️ Blockchain vs Traditional Databases

🏦 Traditional Database

  • Managed by a central administrator (DBA)
  • Data can be modified or deleted by the admin
  • Fast read/write — optimized for performance
  • Requires trust in the organization running it
  • Access controlled by permissions and passwords
  • Single server or cluster — vulnerable to attacks

⛓️ Blockchain

  • Distributed across thousands of nodes globally
  • Immutable — once written, data cannot be altered
  • Slower writes — consensus required across nodes
  • Trustless — math and cryptography replace trust
  • Access controlled by cryptographic keys
  • No single point of failure — extremely resilient
